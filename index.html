Research

Unveiling ionizing photon escape with high-redshift lensed galaxies

My primary research interest is how individual star-forming regions regulate the production and escape of hydrogen-ionizing photons (commonly just ionizing photons or the Lyman continuum (LyC)). This is important to understand because it has significant implications for cosmic reionization (z ~ 6–10), wherein the first generations of stars and active galactic nuclei (AGN) ionized the intergalactic medium (IGM). The relative contribution of stars versus AGN, the role of massive, luminous galaxies versus light, faint ones, and the time evolution of both dichotomies remain unclear.

Directly studying galaxies during reionization is difficult. The galaxies are extremely distant and generally faint, and the increasingly opaque IGM as near as z > 2 typically makes it impossible to directly observe their LyC photons. This means estimates of LyC escape for reionization galaxies must generally rely on proxies for LyC escape like rest-ultraviolet (UV) colors, dust content, ionization state, etc., which do not always have a clear relation to LyC escape. And although the angular diameter evolution is favorable to reionization galaxies, they are typically unresolved, preventing spatially resolved analyses of their interiors, which is critical to understand LyC production and escape methods.

Studying nearby galaxies instead is promising because many are spatially resolved, but they become unresolved by z=0.1, and they are not necessarily fair comparisons to reionization galaxies, typically requiring criteria to select for galaxies analogous to reionization galaxies. These criteria can suffer from biases. Their LyC is also totally inaccessible to ground-based instruments, and the only state-of-the-art instrument that can access their LyC is the Hubble Space Telescope's (HST) Cosmic Origins Spectrograph, which has an on-sky aperture far too large to constrain LyC escape to individual regions of a galaxy, and often a limited wavelength coverage below the Lyman limit.

My work uses high-redshift, gravitationally lensed galaxies at cosmic noon (z ~ 1–3) to offer an optimal middle ground between directly studying reionization and studying local galaxies. These galaxies offer many advantages. They are much closer in time to reionization, so we expect them to generally be more comparable to reionization galaxies. Their foreground lenses magnify their light by as much as tens or hundreds of times, and increase the angular size of the galaxy, routinely permitting the resolution of individual star-forming regions, and in extreme cases, individual stars. At these redshifts, the LyC from these galaxies is directly accessible with the sharp imaging of optical instruments on HST, and the rest-UV through rest-optical is well-captured by optical and near-infrared instruments on ground-based telescopes, offering a complementary wealth of spectral diagnostics constraining outflows, gas conditions, stellar populations, and much more. This combination is a powerful tool to characterize the environments that leak LyC photons. I combine sharp space-based lens modeling from my collaborators with the rest-UV signatures of outflows, nebular emission, and neutral gas morphology to characterize the ISM as it relates to the escape of LyC photons.

Relevant papers: Mainali et al. (2022), Kim et al. (2023), Rivera-Thorsen et al. (2024)

My primary research interest is how the smallest physical mechanisms in galaxies regulate the production and escape of hydrogen-ionizing photons (commonly just ionizing photons or the Lyman continuum (LyC)). This is important to understand because it has significant implications for cosmic reionization, an ancient cosmological epoch wherein the first generations of ionizing sources ionized most of the universe. The relative contribution of different types of ionizing sources and their contribution's time dependence is still unclear.

Directly studying galaxies during reionization (remember: light has a speed limit, so that we observe galaxies how they appeared when they first emitted their light arriving to us) is difficult. Not only are the galaxies extremely distant and often faint, but it is typically impossible to directly observe their ionizing photons. They were mostly spent ionizing the universe. This makes it difficult to predict from their observable characteristics how many ionizing photons they produce and emit. Additionally, these galaxies are typically unresolved on the sky, meaning we can't see their internal structure to understand how internal mechanics of a galaxy affects its ionizing photon production and escape. Galaxies are busy places made with many building blocks interacting in complex manners, meaning it is critical to peer into galactic interiors to robustly understand galaxies.

That wasn't very encouraging to hear. So what else can we do?

Owing to their proximity, nearby galaxies are excellent choices to scrutinize physical processes in galaxies in detail. They are large on the sky, and modern telescopes can easily make out their tiny features and shape. But for these galaxies, it is impossible to detect their ionizing photons with ground-based telescopes since the Earth's atmosphere absorbs their ionizing photons. Space-based telescopes can circumvent this, but apart from space-based telescopes being more difficult to build, deploy, and operate, ultraviolet detectors are technically challenging to construct, which can severely limit the wavelength range of ionizing photons they can detect, and their ability to distinguish small details on the sky. Furthermore, nearby galaxies are often much more evolved than galaxies from reionization, making a direct comparison an 'apples to oranges' one. Just studying nearby galaxies thought to be analogous to those from reionization can help resolve this, but this is still subject to possible biases of what we think makes nearby galaxies analogous to galaxies during reionization.

My work uses distant, gravitationally lensed galaxies to offer an optimal middle ground between directly studing reionization and studying local galaxies. The largest assemblies of mass in the universe, galaxy clusters, can magnify the light from galaxies behind them by tens or hundreds of times, and also 'zoom in' on background galaxies, so that they appear much larger on the sky than they otherwise would. In this manner, it is often possible to study the interiors of lensed galaxies. The great distance to these lensed galaxies also causes a redshift of their light due to the expansion history of the universe. This means that their light stretches to longer wavelengths as it travels to us. This redshift is strong enough for these galaxies that it is feasible to observe their ionizing photons with optical detectors that offer excellent image quality and wavelength coverage. Because these galaxies are so distant, they can make fairer comparisons to galaxies during reionization, since we see them separated by much less time. I combine the superior image quality of space-based telescopes with ground-based spectroscopy, and use the two to pin down the tiny mechanics that make galaxies leak ionizing photons.

Co-author

The Sunburst Arc with JWST: II. Observations of an Eta Carinae Analog at z=2.37

arXiv

5 citations

This is a journal article written by my collaborator Suhyeon Choe, which is the second in a series from Cycle 1 JWST observations of the Sunburst Arc, the brightest known lensed galaxy. Suhyeon examined NIRSpec IFU observations of an extremely compact object of uncertain identity, dubbed 'Godzilla'. Combining an analysis of JWST's emission line detections, previous lens model constraints, and new NIRCam imaging, Suhyeon discovered Godzilla has a highly inhomogeneous, multiphase environment of variable dust and kinematics, concluding Godizlla is likely a non-erupting, luminous blue variable star, and possibly a binary system with a hotter partner star.

Authors: S. Choe, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, H. Dahle, K. Sharon, M. R. Owens, J. R. Rigby, M. B. Bayliss, M. J. Hayes, T. Hutchison, B. Welch, J. Chisholm, M. D. Gladders, G. Khullar, K. Kim

The Sunburst Arc with JWST: III. An Abundance of Direct Chemical Abundances

arXiv

4 citations

My collaborator Brian Welch wrote this journal article; the third in a series based on Cycle 1 JWST observations of the Sunburst Arc, the brightest known gravitationally lensed galaxy. With JWST's exquisite detections of several auroral emission lines and many other nebular emission lines, Brian constrained the metallicity and gas conditions of a multiply-imaged star cluster leaking ionizing photons, discovering a highly enhanced nitrogen content, likely a product of Wolf-Rayet stars, as laid out in the first paper of the series. Otherwise, the chemical abundances are consistent with local, low metallicity H II regions. This work represents a chemical analysis of a single star cluster at cosmic noon with similar fidelity as those in the nearby universe.

Authors: B. Welch, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, J. R. Rigby, T. A. Hutchison, G. M. Olivier, D. A. Berg, K. Sharon, H. Dahle, M. R. Owens, M. B. Bayliss, G. Khullar, J. Chisholm, M. Hayes, K. J. Kim

The Sunburst Arc with JWST: Detection of Wolf-Rayet stars injecting nitrogen into a low-metallicity, z=2.37 proto-globular cluster leaking ionizing photons

arXiv

4 citations

This is a journal article preprint written by my collaborator Emil Rivera-Thorsen. This work is the first product from a Cycle 1 JWST program to observe the Sunburst Arc, the brightest known lensed galaxy. With the new JWST data, Emil reveals the first direct evidence of Wolf-Rayet stars in the high-redshift universe. Directly confirming the presence of Wolf-Rayet stars in a nitrogen-rich clump at high redshift is significant because it implies that Wolf-Rayet stars may also be responsible for the high nitrogen abundance seen by JWST in other early universe galaxies.

Authors: T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, J. Chisholm, B. Welch, J. R. Rigby, T. Hutchison, M. Florian, K. Sharon, S. Choe, H. Dahle, M. B. Bayliss, G. Khullar, M. Gladders, M. Hayes, A. Adamo, M. R. Owens, K. Kim

COOL-LAMPS. VI. Lens Model and New Constraints on the Properties of COOL J1241+2219, a Bright z = 5 Lyman Break Galaxy and its z = 1 Cluster Lens

The Astrophysical Journal

3 citations

This is a journal article written by my collaborator Calvin Klein. In it, he presents a new, HST-based lens model for COOL J1241+2219, a strongly lensed galaxy and (at the time of discovery) the brightest apparent galaxy at z > 5. The sharper HST data resulted in an improved lens model that favored a higher magnification of the galaxy than previously thought and improved constraints on the cluster mass, galaxy stellar mass and star formation rate. The new lens model is a critical ancillary product for other studies with additional imaging and spectroscopy, including from JWST.

Authors: M. Klein, K. Sharon, K. Napier, M. D. Gladders, G. Khullar, M. Bayliss, H. Dahle, M. R. Owens, A. Stark, S. Brownsberger, K. J. Kim, N. Kuchta, G. Mahler, G. Smith, R. Walker, K. Gozman, M. Resolving Clumpy versus Extended Lyα in Strongly Lensed, High-redshift Lyα Emitters

The Astrophysical Journal

1 citation

This is a journal article written by my collaborator Alex Navarre about his work studying a small sample of strongly lensed LAEs at z ~ 4–5. In this work, he used HST photometry to constrain on subgalactic scales the mass, age, dust content, and star formation histories of the young stellar populations responsible for producing Lyα. Interestingly, he found a dichotomy between young LAEs with clumpy Lyα emission and older LAEs with extended, halo-like Lyα emission. Neither morphology showed clear offsets from the stellar continuum.

Authors: A. Navarre, G. Khullar, M. B. Bayliss, H. Dahle, M. Florian, M. Gladders, K. J. Kim, M. R. Owens, J. Rigby, K. Sharon, T. Shibuya, R. Walker

Strongly lensed [O III] emitters at Cosmic Noon with Roman: Characterizing extreme emission line galaxies on star cluster complex scales (100 pc)

arXiv

This is a white paper submitted by my collaborator Keunho Kim to the Roman Core Community Survey, meant to help define the key science goals and strategies of the Roman Space Telescope. In it, we discuss Roman's ability to both revolutionize the discovery of strongly lensed galaxies at cosmic noon (z ~ 1–3) with its sharp imaging, and powerfully constrain the ISM conditions of the individual star-forming regions that gravitational lensing reveals with its grism spectroscopy. This will transform the statistical study of resolved star formation at cosmological distances, especially as it relates to cosmic reionization (z ~ 6–10).

Authors: K. J. Kim, M. B. Bayliss, H. Dahle, T. Hutchison, K. Sharon, G. Mahler, M. R. Owens, J. E. Rhoads

Small Region, Big Impact: Highly Anisotropic Lyman-continuum Escape from a Compact Starburst Region with Extreme Physical Properties

The Astrophysical Journal Letters

20 citations

This is a journal article written by my collaborator Keunho Kim about the Sunburst Arc, a strongly lensed, LyC-leaking galaxy at z = 2.37. He used HST photometry from wideband to narrowband filters spanning the rest-UV to rest-IR to constrain the ISM and stellar population properties of the galaxy's resolved LyC-leaking region, finding the region to be extremely ionized and leaking nearly pure stellar light almost uncontaminated by nebular continuum or dust extinction through a narrow channel. This result highlights the importance of statistical orientation effects when studying LyC escape, which this study reveals can be highly anisotropic.

Authors: K. J. Kim, M. B. Bayliss, J. R. Rigby, M. D. Gladders, J. Chisholm, K. Sharon, H. Dahle, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, M. K. Florian, G. Khullar, G. Mahler, R. Mainali, K. A. Napier, A. Navarre, M. R. Owens, J. Roberson

The Connection Between Galactic Outflows and the Escape of Ionizing Photons

The Astrophysical Journal

20 citations

This is a journal article written by my collaborator Ramesh Mainali about the Sunburst Arc, a z = 2.37 strongly lensed, LyC-leaking galaxy. He used high-resolution, rest-UV spectroscopy to constrain the ages of the galaxy's constituent stellar populations and identify differences in the outflow properties of the LyC-leaking and nonleaking regions, arguing that the strong galactic outflows and stellar winds of the LyC leaker could play a key role in enabling LyC escape.

Authors: R. Mainali, J. R. Rigby, J. Chisholm, M. Bayliss, R. Bordoloi, M. D. Gladders, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, H. Dahle, K. Sharon, M. Florian, S. Sharma, M. R. Owens, K. Kjellgren, K. J. Kim, J. Wayne

Identifying galaxy-lensed Lyman-alpha emitters

Gemini South / GMOS-S

2 hours

This was the first proposal I wrote as a PI, and also my first accepted proposal as a PI or co-I. The goal was to efficiently identify narrowly separated galaxy-galaxy lens candidate systems with favorable signatures for LyC escape. We requesed two-band optical imaging of several targets in excellent seeing conditions with GMOS-S.

Investigators: R. Owens, K. Kim, M. Bayliss, H. Dahle, J. Burns, K. Sharon, G. Smith, M. Klein, N. Kuchta, R. Walker, E. Rivera-Thorsen, G. Mahler, G. Khullar

Co-investigator

Spatially Resolving Highly Ionized Channels of Lyman Continuum and Lyman Alpha Escape on 10's of Parsecs Scales In A Strongly Lensed Galaxy

HST / ACS, WFC3

44 orbits

This is a HST proposal written by my former undergraduate advisor Matt Bayliss to search for spatially resolved Lyα and LyC emission from a strongly lensed galaxy arc at z=2.5. Combined with existing JWST/NIRSpec IFU spectroscopy, these observations will provide a robust picture of the close connection between Lyα and LyC escape and the physical conditions of star-forming regions, and inform future studies of proxies for, and the physics of, LyC escape.

Investigators: M. Bayliss, H. Dahle, M. Florian, M. D. Gladders, T. A. Hutchison, G. Khullar, K. J. Kim, M. R. Owens, J. R. Rigby, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, K. Sharon

Time delay cosmography with strong cluster lenses

HST / ACS, WFC3, JWST / NIRCam

14 orbits (HST), 11.5 hours (JWST)

This is a joint HST-JWST proposal led by my collaborator Håkon Dahle for a sharp, multiwavelength imaging monitoring program of cluster-lensed quasars. Measuring the variability of these quasars provides a unique and independent measurement of the Hubble constant, and the proposed observations are estimated to shrink the existing Hubble constant uncertainty of this method to ~2.1%, paving the way for a competitive measurement with the more canonical methodologies for measuring the Hubble constant. The observations will also provide extensive archival value to quasar studies at cosmic noon and lensing mass distributions.

Investigators: H. Dahle, M. Bayliss, A. P. Cloonan, M. Florian, M. D. Gladders, T. A. Hutchison, G. Khullar, K. J. Kim, G. Mahler, K. Napier, M. R. Owens, J. R. Rigby, T. E. Rivera-Thorsen, K. Sharon, B. Welch

Spatially Resolved Physical Conditions In a Strongly Lensed X-ray Emitting Dwarf Starburst at Cosmic Noon

Magellan / FIRE

21 hours

This is a proposal co-PId by my former undergraduate advisor Matt Bayliss and his graduate student Josh Roberson to study the Phoenix Arc, a strongly lensed, star-forming galaxy arc, and the only known high-redshift dwarf galaxy with detected X-ray emission from its stellar population. This makes it an excellent target to study—in a spatially resolved manner—the connection between young, low-metallicity stellar populations and X-ray binaries at high redshift, which the proposed Magellan/FIRE spectroscopy will accomplish with observations of rest-optical emission lines to diagnose metallicity, chemical abundances, ionization state, and gas conditions.

Investigators: M. Bayliss, J. Roberson, H. Dahle, M. Gladders, G. Khullar, K. Kim, R. Owens, K. Sharon

Resolving Multi-Peaked Lyman-alpha In A Strongly Lensed Galaxy at z=4: Revealing A Candidate Highly Magnified LyC Leaking Galaxy

Gemini North / GMOS-N

3 hours

This is a Gemini Fast Turnaround proposal submitted by my undergraduate advisor Matt Bayliss to obtain higher spectral resolution spectroscopy of a z=4 lensed, candidate LyC-emitting galaxy. In archival lower resolution spectra, the galaxy's Lyman-α profile shows hints of two narrowly separated peaks, and possibly directly escaping Lyman-α flux at the systemic velocity, which are both good signs of escaping ionizing photons. The higher resolution observations will unambiguously reveal the galaxy's Lyman-α profile in detail and answer definitively if the galaxy is a strong candidate for ionizing photon emission.

Investigators: M. Bayliss, A. Navarre, K. Sharon, H. Dahle, R. Owens, M. Gladders, K. Kim

The Partially Ionized Medium around HII Regions -- the low frequency complement

GBT

49 hours

This is a GBT proposal written by my collaborator Pedro Salas. Its purpose is to characterize the partially ionized medium (PIM) around H II regions in the Milky Way with low frequency hydrogen radio recombination lines (RRLs), which will improve density and size measurements of the PIM and clarify its ionization sources. Additional RRLs from helium, carbon, and sulfur will simultaneously constrain colder and hotter phases of gas.

Investigators: P. Salas, K. Emig, M. Owens, D. A. Roshi, L. Anderson

Galaxies Under Construction: Resolved Scaling Relations and Stellar Mass Assembly as Revealed by Lensed Star-Forming Clumps at Cosmic Noon

JWST / NIRCam, NIRSpec

68 hours

This is a Cycle 2 JWST proposal written by my collaborators Michael Florian (PI) and Gourav Khullar (co-PI). It requests comprehensive NIRCam imaging and NIRSpec IFU observations of 8 lensed galaxies. Combined with archival HST imaging, the observations will constrain the metallicities, ages, dust content, ionization states, and much more of approximately 100 star-forming clumps, transforming our understanding of how the building blocks of galaxies construct bulk galactic properties. These observations are the core of the LEGGOS program.

Investigators: M. Florian, R. Bezanson, J. Chisholm, H. Dahle, M. Gladders, T. Hutchison, G. Khullar, K. Kim, G. Mahler, A. Navarre, R. Owens, J. Rigby, T. Rivera-Thorsen, J. Roberson, K. Sharon, I. Shivaei, B. Welch, K. Whitaker
